# DASHBOARD-DEVELOPMENT 
Company: CodTech IT Solutions
Project: Superstore Sales Dashboard (Power BI)
Submitted by: Mahakdeep kaur
Intern ID:CITS753 

My task was to develop a dashboard in Power BI. The main goal was to learn how to take data and present it in a clear and interactive way so that anyone can understand the performance at a glance. I started by importing the dataset into Power BI. Once the data was loaded, I checked the fields and understood what columns were available for building visuals.

The first step I did was to create KPI cards. KPI cards are small visuals that show important numbers directly. In my project, I made KPI cards for Total Sales, Total Profit, and Total Rows (using Row ID). These cards gave me a quick summary of the dataset and helped me see the overall performance without going into details.

After that, I added a Year slicer. A slicer is like a filter that lets you choose a year and see only that year’s data. This made my dashboard interactive. For example, if I select 2019, all the visuals update to show only 2019’s sales, profit, and rows. This is very useful because it allows the user to focus on one time period instead of looking at everything together.

Next, I worked on charts. I created visuals like bar charts and line charts to show trends and comparisons. These charts helped me understand how sales and profit changed over time and across different categories. I also checked how the data looked when filtered by the slicer. The charts updated correctly, which showed that my dashboard was working as expected.

Once the visuals were ready, I focused on arranging and formatting the dashboard. I placed the KPI cards at the top so they are visible immediately. Below them, I arranged the charts neatly. I added a proper title at the top of the dashboard. I also applied formatting like colors, borders, and font sizes to make the dashboard look professional.

Finally, I reviewed everything. I tested the slicer to make sure it filtered correctly. I checked the KPI cards to confirm they were showing the right totals. I looked at the charts to ensure they were displaying the correct values. After these checks, I was satisfied that the dashboard was complete.

In summary, my project steps were: import the dataset, create KPI cards for Total Sales, Total Profit, and Total Rows, add a Year slicer for interactivity, build charts to show trends, and format the layout neatly with a title. This project gave me hands‑on experience in dashboard development using Power BI. It taught me how to combine data, visuals, and formatting to create a professional dashboard that is both informative and easy to use.
